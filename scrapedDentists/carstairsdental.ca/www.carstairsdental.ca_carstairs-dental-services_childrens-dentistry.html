Common Dental Problems in Children

Dental Decay

Children, and even adults, experience tooth decay on a regular basis. A specific type of bacteria thrives in the mouth. Plaque, which is formed by these bacteria and constantly builds up on the teeth, is a thin, sticky film. Starchy foods are perfect for this bacterial film, to get rid of it. As a byproduct of breaking down sugar, the bacteria create acid along with starch and sugar. The tooth enamel, which serves as the tooth's protective covering, is subsequently destroyed by this acid.

Gum Disease

Periodontal disease is another name for gum conditions. Gum irritation caused by plaque and tartar build up at the tooth's root is known as gingivitis. The child's gums may at first appear red and irritated and may bleed when being brushed and flossed.

Sensitive Teeth

A child with sensitive teeth experiences discomfort when eating or drinking cold or hot things. Teeth sensitivity suggests a more serious issue. Compared to adults, children's enamel is thinner. Plaque and germs can wear it down even further.

Grinding

Children of school age frequently experience bruxism, or teeth grinding. Because the top teeth are not in line with the lower teeth, grinding takes place. Some young children clench their teeth in response to ear aches or pain in the teeth caused by teething. Bruxism typically disappears as a child gets older. However, if it continues, it could wear down permanent teeth, which could lead to worn-out enamel resulting in pain and sensitive teeth. Your child can stop grinding their teeth with the aid of a night guard.

Baby Teeth Fall Out

The first front middle tooth typically becomes loose and falls out around the age of six. However, occasionally the tooth does not fall out. When a tooth falls out too soon or does not fall out at all, the dentist should be consulted.

Frequently Asked Questions

About Children Dentistry

When Should I Bring My Child for a Dental Appointment?

The main goals of first visits are to acclimatize children to the dental chair and inform parents on how to care for their infant’s teeth. You receive a one-year pass till the age of two if your child has switched from the bottle to a cup and doesn’t snack or drink in the middle of the night. The conventional advice to visit the dentist every six months then comes into play. Expect your dentist to take the first set of X-rays when your child is between the ages of 4 and 6 to look for cavities that may be hiding between the teeth.

Between the ages of 6 and 12, when baby teeth are replaced by permanent teeth, prevention is key. Between the ages of 7 and 9, your child’s dentist may  advise a sealant, a plastic resin that adheres to the chewing surfaces of teeth. The most likely area for treatment is a molar that is prone to cavities. The glue prevents bacteria that cause cavities from entering the troughs and grooves of teeth.

What Should I Expect for the First Visit?

First visits typically take between 30 and 45 minutes. The visit could include a thorough examination of your child’s teeth, jaws, bite, gums, and oral tissues to assess growth and development, depending on their age. Your child might also get a light cleaning if necessary.

How to Prevent Bad Dental Habits?

While some people may need to pay extra attention to their oral health, everyone should practice good oral hygiene. Here are some tips to get you on the road to good oral health:

  • Visit your dentist one to two times a year for a cleaning and checkup.
  • Brush your teeth with fluoride toothpaste a minimum of two times per day.
  • Replace your toothbrush or toothbrush head every three to four months.
  • Floss at least once per day.
  • Brush your tongue to remove bacteria and freshen your breath.
  • Some people will benefit from fluoride treatments and mouth rinses.
Are X-rays Safe for Children?

We understand that you might feel worried about exposing your youngster to radiation. However, X-ray technology has advanced significantly and is now more secure than ever! X-rays are an important tool for both adults and children in the diagnosis and prevention of a variety of dental problems.

Do Baby Teeth Really Matter?

Baby teeth are significant because they play a crucial role in the development of adult teeth. The “paths” made by the baby teeth are followed by the adult teeth to form the correct alignment.

In essence, baby teeth serve as spacers to aid in the proper emergence of adult teeth into the mouth. Premature tooth loss might affect your child’s dental alignment and interfere with their future oral development.

Therefore, it’s crucial to obtain help, like space maintainers, if your child loses one or more of their baby teeth early in order to retain the right gaps in their smile and reduce any potential difficulties with oral growth.

How Can I Protect My Child's Baby Teeth?

According to your dentist’s suggestions, schedule routine dental checkups for children as they develop ranging from once every three months to once a year. To maintain good dental health, limit their intake of sugary foods, encourage frequent brushing and flossing, and cooperate with your dentist.

Will My Child Need Braces?

Your child might need braces if he/she has the following conditions:

  • Crooked teeth
  • A history of thumb sucking
  • Gaps between the teeth
  • The upper teeth overlap the lower teeth too much
  • The lower teeth cover the upper teeth
  • Breathing through the mouth
  • Your child often complains about the mouth pain
  • Cheek biting
How Long Should a Child Visit a Dentist?

A dentist normally treats kids from the time they are six months old until all of their adult teeth have come in. Many parents decide to continue bringing their kids to a pediatric dentistry professional well into adolescence.

Is It Normal for Babies to Have a White Tongue?

Having a white tongue is pretty typical for newborns. Babies frequently develop a milk deposit on their tongues, although it could also be a sign of oral thrush.

What Is the Importance of Milk Teeth?

A child needs these “milk teeth” in order to chew food and communicate. However, baby teeth also play a very significant role in preserving room for a child’s eventual permanent teeth. A baby tooth typically stays in a child’s mouth until the underlying permanent tooth is ready to break through the gums.

Should I Clean My Baby’s Mouth if There Are No Teeth?

Even though the majority of infants do not begin to grow teeth until they are 6 months old, it is advised to start cleaning the baby’s mouth as soon as they are born, even before the teeth come in.

When Should a Child Start Brushing?

Brush your child’s teeth gently with water and a soft children’s toothbrush as soon as the first tooth erupts (often at around 6 months of age). To clean the teeth and gums, you might also use a soft, damp towel. Twice daily, before getting into bed at night and in the morning, brush your child’s teeth and gums on all surfaces.
