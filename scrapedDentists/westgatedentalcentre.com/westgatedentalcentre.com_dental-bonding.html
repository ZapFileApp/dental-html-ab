Dental bonding is an advanced method of cosmetic and restorative dentistry. Whether it is structural damage or just a matter of aesthetics, this method can fix it all by applying tooth-coloured composite resin to the tooth’s surface. 

Correct application and curing procedures are essential for successful restoration and enhancement. This cosmetic dental procedure can restore your smile if you have broken, stained, chipped or cracked teeth.

Westgate Dental & Implant Centre provides a professionally administered dental bonding procedure for Maple Ridge residents. Explore our professional steps and how they can enhance your oral health and appearance.

Advantages

Tooth Discolouration

  • Composite resin covers intrinsic stains and discolored teeth. 
  • Resin is cured in place and matched to the shade.
  • Hides discoloration without veneers or crowns. 
  • Can be colour-matched to surrounding teeth for a uniform look.

Uneven Tooth Shape or Size

  • Applying resin can repair chipped teeth. 
  • Can be sculpted to the correct size or desired shape
  • For patients seeking non-invasive tooth form changes. 
  • Helps correct chipped tooth, minor shape discrepancies and improve symmetry.

Small Gaps Between Teeth

  • Fill small interdental gaps with composite resin. 
  • The resin is applied to the teeth next to the gap to close it and create a continuous surface.
  • This gap-closing method is fast and cheap. 
  • Takes one appointment and is less invasive than dental implants and orthodontics.

Minor Tooth Misalignment

  • Straightens sharp edges for slight misalignments. 
  • Applying resin to specific areas improves alignment and appearance.
  • Addresses minor alignment issues non-orthodontically. 
  • Improves appearance immediately and is often used with orthodontic or cosmetic procedures.

Our Cosmetic Dental Bonding Process

Clinical Evaluation and Preparation

  • Clinical Assessment. A thorough exam determines if the treatment is suitable for the patient. Our dentists assess tooth structure, damage, and oral health.
  • Material Choice. Composite resin is chosen to match the patient’s teeth’s colour and translucency. Resin selection affects aesthetics and function.
  • Enamel Preparation. A conditioning agent roughens enamel to prepare the tooth surface. Our surface modification improves agent adhesion. Conditioning with an acid-etching gel creates a microporous surface that reinforces mechanical retention.

Application of the Composite Material

  • Resin Application. Layers of composite resin are applied to the tooth’s surface. Each layer is carefully sculpted to match tooth contours, and sculpting tools help our dentist achieve precise results.
  • Shaping and Contouring. Models resins to fix chips, cracks, cavities, and gaps. Malleable material allows detailed sculpting to match natural tooth structure.

Bonding and Curing

  • Light Curing. Polymerization begins with a blue LED curing light directed at the resin. The resin hardens and bonds to the tooth when the curing light activates its photoinitiators.
  • Chemical Adhesion. A durable bond is formed when resins react with tooth structure. The curing process maximizes the resin’s strength and adhesion.

Final Adjustments and Polishing

After curing, our dentists in Maple Ridge trim and adjust the bonded material for a natural look and proper alignment. We refine the shape and the surface of the tooth. Polishing the bonded tooth gives it a glossy finish. It also improves stain resistance and the aesthetic integration of the bonded material with natural teeth.

Materials and Tools

  1. Composite Resin. Composites employ a mixture of resins and fillers, which resemble enamel and dentin.
  2. Conditioning Agents. Usually phosphoric acid-based, these agents micro-etch broken teeth to improve adhesion.
  3. Curing Light. A device that emits specific light wavelengths to cure resin. Halogen and LED lights are standard.
  4. Sculpting and Polishing Tools. Include hand tools and polishing materials for shaping and smoothing bonded resin.

Aftercare and Maintenance

  1. Good Oral Hygiene Practices. Always floss after brushing and use non-abrasive toothpaste. To avoid damaging the bonded areas, use a toothbrush with soft bristles.
  2. Stain Prevention. Drinking too much coffee, tea, or tobacco can leave unsightly stains. A quick rinse with water will help minimize the staining potential after consumption.
  3. Avoid Hard Objects. Refrain from biting hard objects like ice or pens, which could damage the bonding materials. Consider using a nightguard to prevent a broken tooth.
  4. Regular Check-ups. Schedule dentist visits for routine examinations and cleanings. Our dentist will assess the condition of the bonded teeth and address any issues promptly.
  5. Prompt Attention to Concerns. Notify your dentist right away if you notice any changes or issues with the bonded teeth, including roughness, discoloration, or pain.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is there anything I should not eat after getting teeth bonding?

Yes, these are the things you should not eat following a teeth-bonding procedure.

  • Hard Foods. Can crack or chip the dental bonding material.
  • Sticky Foods. Can pull at and dislodge the composite bonding.
  • Staining Foods. Can stain the composite resin material.
  • Acidic Foods. Can erode resins and weaken them.

How long does bonding material last?

Although this can vary, in most cases, it lasts between five and ten years before needing replacement. The longevity of a composite depends on the resin material.

Resins with greater strength and durability are usually placed further back into the mouth because they do not look natural. For front applications, your dentist may use a tooth-coloured resin that resembles natural teeth.

How does tooth bonding compare to other treatments?

  • Veneers: Thin porcelain veneers or composite shells that cover the front surface of teeth, offering superior durability and stain resistance. While cosmetic bonding is minimally invasive, veneers require more extensive preparation and are commonly more expensive.
  • Crowns: Provide full coverage and are ideal for severely damaged teeth. Offer enhanced strength and protection but necessitate substantial removal of enamel structure. Crowns are also more costly and time-consuming to fabricate.

The choice between bonding and other cosmetic dental procedures depends on factors such as the extent of your mouth issues, aesthetic goals, and budget constraints.
