Serving Maple Ridge and Surrounding Areas 

Root canal treatment is a general dentistry procedure addressing infections deep in a tooth’s pulp to prevent tooth loss and complications. In Canada, more than 15 million teeth are cared for and saved yearly with root canal treatment.

Ready to trust Westgate Dental & Implant Centre with your dental worries?

What is a Root Canal Procedure?

Root canal treatments remove infected or damaged pulp tissue from the passages, followed by disinfection, shaping, and sealing. According to a study published in Cureus (2023), root canal therapy has a success index of over 90%, which makes it an effective dental procedure for your aching tooth.

First, let’s take a look at the three main layers:

Enamel

The outermost, hardest layer of the structure.

Dentin

The layer below the enamel which is less dense but still provides structural support.

Pulp

The innermost layer containing nerves, blood vessels, and soft tissue.

What are the Causes of Inflammation and Infection?

Deep Dental Caries (Decay)

Carious lesions result from prolonged bacterial activity that demineralizes the structure. When decay progresses beyond the enamel and dentin, it can invade the chamber.

  • Bacteria in the carious dentin produce acids that lead to pulpitis, an inflammation of the pulp. If untreated, this can result in contamination or necrosis (death of the tissue).

Large Fillings

Extensive restorations covering a large part may lead to irritation or infection.

  • Large fillings increase the risk of exposure during the placement or may exert pressure on the remaining ones for inflammation. 

Periodontal Disease

Severe gum disease can affect the surrounding bone and tissues, forming periodontal pockets.

  • Periodontal disease can create pathways for bacteria to reach the pulp through the pits, causing pulpitis or abscess formation.

Repeated Procedures

Multiple restorative treatments, such as fillings or crowns, can expose the pulp or irritate it.

  • Repeated mechanical and chemical stresses can cause inflammation or infection.

Cracks or Chips

Surface cracks or chips can provide a pathway for bacteria to enter the chamber.

  • These defects can allow bacteria to penetrate the tooth structure, leading to infection. 

Signs You May Need Root Canal Treatment

Persistent Toothache

Often described as throbbing or severe pain that may be spontaneous or aggravated by chewing or pressure—an indicator of pulpitis or necrosis.

Prolonged Sensitivity

Hypersensitivity to hot or cold stimuli that lingers after the stimulus is removed.

Discolouration

A darkening or discolouration can be indicative of necrosis or internal bleeding.

Swelling and Tenderness

Swelling of the gums or surrounding tissues and tenderness or pain on palpation may indicate an abscess or pathogen that has spread to the periapical tissues.

Presence of a Pimple on the Gums

A small, pimple-like swelling on the gums, known as a fistula, can signify an abscess, often resulting from an affliction. 

Beware of the Consequences

  1. An untreated infection can lead to an abscess, a cluster of pus that causes severe pain and swelling.
  2. Severe contamination or necrosis weakens the tooth, making it more susceptible to fracture or loss. 

How Our Maple Ridge Endodontist Performs the Root Canal Treatment

Diagnosis and Pre-Operative Evaluation

The patient may present symptoms such as pain, sensitivity to hot and cold, or swelling. These clinical signs, along with a visual inspection, provide our dentist in Maple Ridge with an insight into its condition.

  • Digital radiography or cone-beam computed tomography (CBCT) examines the internal structures.
  • Radiographs allow our dentist to visualize the scope of bacteria, the presence of periapical pathology (such as abscesses or cysts), and the complexity of the pit.
  • CBCT provides 3D images for anatomical assessment.

Anesthesia and Isolation

We begin with administering local anesthesia to numb the affected area and surrounding tissues. Lidocaine with epinephrine is commonly used while controlling bleeding.

  • A rubber dam is placed to isolate it from saliva and maintain a dry, sterile field, preventing contamination by oral bacteria and ensuring that irrigants do not enter the mouth.

Access Cavity Preparation

Our dentist creates an access cavity by drilling through the enamel and dentin to reach the chamber. During this stage:

  • A high-speed handpiece drill is used to create the access opening.
  • Once the chamber is exposed, the infected or necrotic tissue is removed using endodontic instruments.

Cleaning and Shaping

Stainless steel or nickel-titanium (NiTi) endodontic files mechanically clean and shape the canals. Files range in size and taper to accommodate anatomies.

  • Throughout the cleaning, the canals are irrigated with sodium hypochlorite (NaOCl), chlorhexidine, or EDTA (ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid).
  • Irrigation dissolves tissue remnants, eliminates bacteria, and removes the smear layer (a thin layer of debris created during mechanical instrumentation).
  • Our dentist shapes the canals, tapering them towards the apex (tip) to facilitate obturation (sealing).

Disinfection

Sodium hypochlorite is the gold-standard irrigant due to its tissue-dissolving and antimicrobial properties. Other solutions, like chlorhexidine and EDTA, complement the disinfection by targeting different bacterial species and removing mineralized tissue.

  • In some cases, ultrasonic activation of the irrigants is used to enhance penetration into a root canal system. Ultrasonic waves help agitate the irrigants, improving their efficacy in cleaning intricate spaces.

Obturation

Gutta-Percha

A thermoplastic material that is biocompatible, inert, and can be compacted into the shaped canals.

Sealer Application

Before inserting the gutta-percha, an endodontic sealer (a resin—or calcium hydroxide-based material) coats the walls.

Lateral or Vertical Compaction

Gutta-percha is inserted into the canal using either lateral compaction, where small accessory cones compact the material, or vertical compaction, where heated instruments soften and press the material tightly into the space.

Apical Seal

Ensuring a seal at the apex prevents bacteria from re-entering through the tip.

Temporary or Permanent Restoration

  • If a follow-up procedure, such as placing a crown, is required, a temporary filling like Cavit or IRM is placed in the access cavity. This provides a short-term seal until the permanent restoration can be completed.
  • In most cases, canal-treated teeth require a permanent restoration, such as a crown, to protect them from fracture. Teeth that undergo treatment are more brittle due to the removal of the tissue, and a crown restores function and durability.

What are the Benefits of Root Canal Treatment?

Natural Tooth Preservation

Extracted teeth need to be replaced to restore chewing, which often requires implants, bridges, or dentures. The endodontic treatment eliminates the need for replacement by preserving the adjacent teeth.

  • When a part is extracted, the bone supporting it (alveolar bone) begins to resorb (shrink) over time due to a lack of stimulation. The treatment prevents bone loss by maintaining the natural ones.
  • Natural retainment, especially in the front of the mouth, preserves the smile’s appearance and facial structure. Even when crowns or restorations are used post-treatment, the natural base and surrounding bone maintain a more authentic look than prosthetics.

Prevention of Pathogen Spread

An abscess forms when the pathogen spreads to the periapical tissue (the connective tissue surrounding the base apex), resulting in pus accumulation, which can cause severe pain, swelling, and fever. The procedure removes the source and prevents the development of an abscessed tooth or worsening of abscesses.

  • In extreme cases, it may spread into the jawbone, leading to osteomyelitis (bone infection). By addressing it early through therapy, the spread to surrounding structures, including the bone, can be avoided.
  • Untreated ones can lead to bacteremia (bacteria entering the bloodstream) and systemic health problems. Early intervention kills the source, reducing the risk of complications.

Pain Relief

Removing the infected tooth, which contains nerve endings, eliminates the source of pain. Following the procedure, you can expect relief from the intense discomfort caused by the inflamed or infected pulp tissue.

  • A unit undergoing intervention may be highly sensitive to temperature changes or pressure. However, sensitivity can be reduced by proper cleaning and removing the damaged part.

Preservation of Bite Force and Chewing Efficiency

Preserving natural teeth helps maintain proper masticatory (chewing) function, which is vital for digestion and nutrition.

  • Maintaining the natural one prevents the shifting of other teeth, preserving proper occlusion (bite alignment). Shifts in position due to extractions can lead to malocclusion, temporomandibular joint (TMJ) disorders, and other complications.

Root Canal Aftercare and Recovery

Post-operative Discomfort and Pain Management

To manage discomfort, over-the-counter pain relievers such as ibuprofen (a nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug, NSAID) or acetaminophen are recommended. Ibuprofen is effective due to its anti-inflammatory attributes.

  • Using a cold compress to the affected area for short intervals (15–20 minutes) immediately after the procedure can reduce swelling and numb the area for pain relief.

Avoiding Pressure

Our patients are advised to stick to soft foods such as soups, yogurt, and mashed vegetables for the first few days. Hard, crunchy, or gooey foods should be avoided to prevent damage or temporary restoration.

Oral Hygiene Practices

Our patients should continue brushing twice daily using a soft-bristled toothbrush and fluoride toothpaste. Gentle brushing is recommended to avoid irritation.

  • Regular flossing should be continued, but care must be taken not to dislodge the temporary root canal filling material. Flossing is needed to remove food particles and plaque.
  • An antimicrobial or chlorhexidine mouthwash can help reduce the bacterial load in the mouth, providing an extra layer of protection against reinfection.

Long-Term Care

Regular visits to our dentist (every six months) help assess the health of the treated tooth and ensure that the restoration remains intact. During these visits, our dentist may take radiographs to check for any signs of reinfection or changes in the surrounding bone.

  • Crowns or permanent fillings should be inspected regularly for wear, cracks, or signs of deterioration.
  • Patients who grind their teeth (bruxism) are at higher risk of damaging a treated unit. An occlusal guard (night guard) may be recommended for patients with a history of bruxism.

Lifestyle

Reducing the intake of sweet treats and beverages is key to maintaining oral health.

  • Smoking has been shown to impact healing negatively, and it can increase the likelihood of complications.

What are the Potential Risks?

Post-Treatment Infection

Accessories and branches could be difficult to access and clean thoroughly. Bacteria left in these areas can proliferate, reinfecting the area.

  • If the coronal (top) portion is not properly sealed, bacteria from the cavity may cause reinfection.
  • While the pulp may have been removed, the surrounding dentin and cementum can still harbour bacteria if exposed due to fractures.

Instrument Breakage

Canals are rarely straight; many are curved, especially in molars. Files can become lodged in these curves and fracture under tension. The broken piece of the file can remain inside the canal, complicating the cleaning and leading to failure if not retrieved.

  • In older patients or teeth with a history of trauma, the canals become calcified, narrowing the space and increasing the risk of file separation. 

Perforation

Crown Perforation – This can occur if the cavity is prepared too aggressively or in the wrong location, resulting in a hole through the crown.

Root Perforation – This occurs when the instrument used to clean or shape the canals goes beyond the confines of the base, damaging the surrounding periodontal ligament or bone.

Treatment of Perforations – In many cases, perforations can be repaired using biocompatible materials such as mineral trioxide aggregate (MTA) or biodentine.

Post-operative Pain and Inflammation

If excess instrumentation or overfilling occurs during the procedure, the periapical tissue (located around the tip) becomes irritated.

  • Swelling can occur due to the body’s immune response to the procedure. In some cases, minor swelling is expected, but more severe swelling indicates contamination or abscess that requires immediate attention.
  • A flare-up is an acute exacerbation of discomfort and swelling within a few days after the procedure. It can be caused by a sudden immune response to remaining bacterial debris or incomplete cleaning.
  • Flare-ups, though rare (around 1-6% of cases), can be managed with pain relief medication, anti-inflammatory drugs, or antibiotics if a contagion is suspected.

Frequently Asked Question

What is the average number of root canals per tooth?

The number varies by type and location. On average:

  • Incisors (front teeth): 1
  • Premolars (bicuspids): 2
  • Molars (back teeth): 3-4, with some lower molars having up to 5.

What are the typical recovery times after root canal treatment?

  • Initial Recovery: Most patients experience a reduction in pain and discomfort within 1-3 days post-treatment.
  • Full Healing: Complete resolution of symptoms and full healing of the periapical tissues take 2-6 months.

What is the incidence of accidental perforation during root canal treatment?

Accidental perforation occurs in about 2-5% of procedures. Perforations can happen during access or instrumentation and require repair with mineral trioxide aggregate (MTA). Early detection and appropriate management mitigate the impact of perforations on the treatment outcome.
