Serving Maple Ridge and Surrounding Areas 

Sleep apnea is a disorder marked by repeated interruptions in breathing during sleep. Affecting 6.4% of Canadians, it not only disrupts rest but also impacts overall health, leading to conditions such as hypertension, heart disease, and diabetes.

In line with Health Canada’s guidelines, untreated sleep apnea may increase the risk of cardiovascular complications.

Westgate Dental & Implant Centre integrates innovations in sleep apnea treatment to provide our patients with healthier rest patterns and prevent other sleep disorders.  

Understanding Sleep Apnea and Its Types

These lulls in breathing, known as apneas, can last from a few seconds to minutes and occur multiple times throughout the night.

Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA)

The most common form, obstructive sleep apnea, occurs when the rear muscles of the throat relax excessively, blocking the airway. This prevents air from reaching the lungs despite ongoing efforts to breathe.

Central Sleep Apnea (CSA)

CSA is rare and involves the brain failing to transmit signals to the breathing muscles. Unlike OSA, there is no physical blockage; instead, the brain fails to signal the muscles to breathe.

Complex Sleep Apnea Syndrome

Otherwise known as treatment-emergent central cessation—a fusion of OSA and CSA. It is identified when central apneas persist despite using a CPAP machine for OSA.

Sleep Apnea Symptoms

  • Loud snoring
  • Stop breathing during sleep
  • Abrupt awakenings
  • Morning headache
  • Excessive daytime sleepiness

Risk Factors

Obesity

Excess weight around the neck contributes to airway obstruction.

Age

Cessation is more common in older adults.

Gender

Men are more prone to develop this disorder than women.

Family History

Genetics can play a role in the risk of acquiring one.

Use of Alcohol or Sedatives

These relax the throat muscles, increasing the chance of airway obstruction.

Smoking

This increases inflammation and fluid retention in the upper airway. 

Role of Dental Professionals in Treatment

Identification and Screening

Dentists examine the oropharyngeal anatomy, including the size and position of the tongue, uvula, soft palate, and tonsils. Enlarged tonsils, a high-arched palate, or a retrognathic jaw (recessed lower jaw) suggest a predisposition to OSA.

  • Patients may report symptoms such as bruxism (teeth grinding), xerostomia (dry mouth), or daytime fatigue. Observing these signs prompts further investigation.
  • Approximately 50% of patients exhibit bruxism. Dental visits with a general dentistry are necessary for early detection. 

Consultation

Our dentists Maple Ridge asks specific questions to understand your symptoms, lifestyle, and sleep apnea treatment options.

Symptom Assessment

  • How often do you snore, and how loud is it?
  • Do you wake up during the night feeling short of breath or gasping for air?
  • Do you feel excessively sleepy or fatigued during the day, even after a full night’s rest?

Bed Habits

  • How many hours of rest do you get on average, and do you feel rested upon waking?
  • Is your environment conducive to restful slumber (e.g., dark, quiet, and comfortable)?

Medical and Lifestyle Factors

  • Have you experienced drastic weight gain or loss recently?
  • Do you have any medical conditions such as hypertension, diabetes, or cardiovascular disease?
  • Are you currently taking any medications that may affect your rest?

Family History and Risk Factors

  • Does anyone in your family have this disorder or related sleep disorders?
  • Do you smoke, consume alcohol, or have a sedentary lifestyle?

Polysomnography (PSG)

Polysomnography, or a sleep study, is the benchmark for diagnosis. This is conducted overnight in our lab and involves:

  1. Electroencephalogram (EEG) measures brain wave activity to identify different stages.
  2. Electrooculogram (EOG) tracks eye movements to determine the transition between REM and non-REM.
  3. Electromyogram (EMG) records muscle activity in the chin and legs to detect muscle tone and movements.
  4. Electrocardiogram (ECG) monitors heart rate and rhythm.
  5. Pulse Oximetry measures blood oxygen levels to detect drops associated with apneas.
  6. Respiratory Effort Sensors are placed on the chest and abdomen to record breathing effort.
  7. Airflow Sensors are positioned near the nose and mouth to measure airflow and detect interruptions.

Home Sleep Apnea Testing (HSAT)

For patients who may find an overnight lab study inconvenient, HSAT is an alternative. Our patient uses portable monitoring equipment at home to measure:

  1. Airflow detected through nasal cannula.
  2. Respiratory Effort measured by chest and abdominal belts.
  3. Blood Oxygen Levels monitored using a finger pulse oximeter.
  4. Heart Rate tracked through a pulse oximeter.

Interpreting Results

The results from polysomnography or HSAT are analyzed to determine the Apnea-Hypopnea Index (AHI), which quantifies its severity:

Mild Sleep Apnea

AHI of 5-15 events per hour.

Moderate Sleep Apnea

AHI of 15-30 events per hour.

Severe Sleep Apnea

AHI of more than 30 events per hour.

Treating Sleep Apnea

Lifestyle Modifications

For our patients with mild obstructive sleep apnea or those looking to complement other treatments, lifestyle changes reduce symptoms.

  • Studies show that dropping just 10% of body mass decreases the severity by 20%.
  • Regular physical activity helps improve respiratory function and overall health, reducing its symptoms.
  • Limiting alcohol and sedative intake, especially before bedtime, can help.
  • Some of our patients experience episodes when resting on their backs. Positional therapy, which involves lying on one’s side, can be beneficial. Special devices and pillows are available to help maintain side positions.

Continuous Positive Airway Pressure (CPAP) Therapy

CPAP involves wearing a mask (covering the nose and mouth) during rest. Delivering a continuous stream of air pressure, keeping a patent airway and preventing apneas.

  • The pressure is set based on the severity, ranging from 5 to 20 cm H2O.
  • Various masks, including nasal masks, full-face masks, and nasal pillows, are available to ensure comfort and compliance.
  • Modern CPAP machines, i.e., bilevel positive airway pressure, offer features such as ramp settings (gradually increasing pressure), humidifiers (to prevent dryness), and data tracking (for monitoring therapy effectiveness).

Oral Appliance Therapy

This therapy is a viable alternative for patients with mild to moderate OSA or those who cannot tolerate CPAP. These custom-fitted devices, similar to mouthguards, reposition the lower jaw and tongue to maintain a permeable airway.

  • Mandibular Advancement Devices (MADs) move the lower jaw forward, increasing the space behind the tongue and reducing airway obstruction.
  • Tongue-retaining devices hold the tongue in a front position to prevent it from collapsing back into the airway.
  • Oral appliances are custom-made based on dental impressions, ensuring a precise fit and maximum effectiveness.
  • Studies show that oral appliances are effective in reducing AHI (Apnea-Hypopnea Index) by 50% or more in many patients.

Surgical Options

For severe obstructive sleep apnea or when anatomical abnormalities contribute to airway obstruction, dentists may recommend surgical interventions, like:

  • Uvulopalatopharyngoplasty (UPPP) widens the airway by removing excess tissue from the throat, including the uvula, part of the soft palate, and the tonsils.
  • Genioglossus Advancement (GA) repositions the tongue muscle attachment forward. Prevents the tongue from collapsing backward and obstructing the airway.
  • Maxillomandibular Advancement (MMA) moves both the upper and lower jaws forward for upper airway stimulation. It is ideal for patients with severe OSA and craniofacial abnormalities.
  • Inspire Therapy implants a small device that stimulates the hypoglossal nerve. Controls tongue movements and is activated during sleep to maintain airway patency.

Benefits of Treating Sleep Apnea

Improved Sleep Quality

Studies show that patients using CPAP therapy experience a 50-75% reduction in the Apnea-Hypopnea Index (AHI), improving rest quality.

Reduced Risk of Metabolic Disorders

Cessation is associated with insulin resistance and an increased risk of type 2 diabetes. CPAP therapy improves insulin sensitivity by 25%, reducing the risk of developing type 2 diabetes.

Cardiovascular Health Benefits

Continuous Positive Airway Pressure (CPAP) therapy prevents apnea-related dips in blood oxygen levels (hypoxemia) and reduces sympathetic nervous system engagement, which lowers blood pressure and decreases the risk of arrhythmias.

Enhanced Mood and Mental Health

CPAP and other treatments help normalize levels of neurotransmitters (serotonin and dopamine) that regulate mood. It also reduces cortisol levels, mitigating stress and anxiety.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the success ratio of oral appliance therapy?

Oral appliance therapy has a success rate of 60-70% for our patients with mild to moderate obstructive sleep apnea (OSA). These devices reduce the Apnea-Hypopnea Index (AHI) by at least 50% in many patients.

How frequently should oral appliances be adjusted or replaced?

Oral appliances should be evaluated and adjusted at least once a year to ensure optimal fit and function. However, adjustments may be needed more frequently during the initial phase of treatment, every 3-6 months.

Most oral appliances last 3-5 years, after which they should be replaced to maintain effectiveness and hygiene.

How many cases are diagnosed annually in Canada?

An estimated 5.4 million Canadians are diagnosed, with an annual increase of around 300,000 new cases—highlighting the growing awareness and diagnosis for effective treatment.
