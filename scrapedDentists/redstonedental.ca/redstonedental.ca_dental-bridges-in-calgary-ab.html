What Are Dental Bridges?

A dental bridge is a fixed dental restoration designed to “bridge” the gap created by one or more missing teeth. It consists of artificial teeth called pontics that fill the empty space, supported by dental crowns or frameworks attached to adjacent natural teeth or dental implants.

The bridge components work together to create a seamless restoration:

Bridge Components

  • Pontic (Artificial Tooth): The replacement tooth or teeth that fill the gap, typically made from porcelain, ceramic, or porcelain-fused-to-metal for a natural appearance.

  • Abutment Teeth: The natural teeth or dental implants on either side of the gap that serve as anchors for the bridge.

  • Crowns or Framework: Depending on the bridge type, dental crowns placed over abutment teeth or a metal/porcelain framework bonded to adjacent teeth.

Unlike removable dentures that can slip or require adhesives, dental bridges are permanently cemented, providing stability and function similar to natural teeth. According to the Canadian Dental Association, bridges have been successfully used for decades to restore smiles and maintain proper bite alignment.

Types of Dental Bridges

Each type of dental bridge offers unique advantages depending on the missing teeth and the health of the surrounding teeth.

Traditional Dental Bridge

The most common type, traditional bridges, use dental crowns placed on natural teeth on both sides of the gap to support one or more pontics between them. This option is ideal when you have healthy, natural teeth adjacent to the missing tooth space. Traditional bridges are strong enough to replace molars and back teeth that endure significant chewing pressure.

Cantilever Dental Bridge

A cantilever bridge is supported by a crown on only one adjacent tooth rather than both sides. This design is functional when only one natural tooth is available next to the gap, such as at the back of the mouth. However, because the bridge is anchored on just one side, it places more force on the supporting tooth and is generally not recommended for areas with heavy chewing pressure.

Maryland Dental Bridge

Also called a resin-bonded bridge, Maryland bridges use a metal or porcelain framework bonded to the back of adjacent teeth rather than requiring full crowns. This conservative approach preserves more natural tooth structure, making it ideal for replacing front teeth. Maryland bridges are less invasive but may not be strong enough for molars or back teeth.

Implant-Supported Dental Bridge

Instead of relying on natural teeth, implant-supported bridges are anchored by dental implants surgically placed in the jawbone. This option is recommended when you have multiple missing teeth in a row or when adjacent teeth aren’t healthy enough to support a traditional bridge. Implant-supported bridges offer superior stability and help prevent jawbone loss.

Why Are Dental Bridges Important?

Replacing missing teeth goes far beyond cosmetic concerns; it is essential for maintaining oral health, facial structure, and overall quality of life.

Prevents Tooth Migration

When a tooth is lost, neighboring teeth naturally drift or tilt into the space. This shifting can lead to bite misalignment, jaw joint problems, and difficulty cleaning between crooked teeth, which increases your risk of decay and gum disease. Dental bridges fill the gap and keep surrounding teeth in their proper positions.

Maintains Facial Structure and Prevents Bone Loss

Missing teeth causes the jawbone in that area to deteriorate over time due to a lack of stimulation. This bone loss can result in a sunken facial appearance and premature aging. While traditional bridges don’t prevent bone loss like implants, they help maintain facial contours by supporting surrounding structures.

Restores Chewing Function and Speech

Gaps in your smile make it challenging to chew certain foods properly, potentially affecting your nutrition and digestive health. Missing teeth can also impact speech, causing lisping or difficulty pronouncing certain sounds. Dental bridges restore nearly normal chewing ability and improve speech clarity, allowing you to eat various foods and communicate confidently.

Distributes Bite Forces Evenly

When teeth are missing, the remaining teeth must absorb extra pressure during chewing, which can lead to excessive wear, cracks, and fractures over time. Bridges distribute bite forces evenly across your dental arch, protecting your remaining natural teeth from damage.

Who Needs Dental Bridges?

Dental bridges are an excellent tooth replacement option for many people, but determining candidacy requires professional evaluation. You may be a good candidate for dental bridges if you:

  • Have one to three consecutive missing teeth in the same area​.
  • Possess healthy, strong teeth adjacent to the gap to serve as abutments (for traditional bridges).
  • Have adequate bone structure (if considering implant-supported bridges).
  • Maintain good oral hygiene and are committed to keeping the bridge clean​.
  • Have healthy gums free from active periodontal disease​.
  • Seek a fixed (non-removable) tooth replacement solution.
  • Want a faster, less invasive alternative to implant placement

When Additional Treatment May Be Needed

Certain conditions may require preliminary care before bridge placement. If you have untreated gum disease, tooth decay in adjacent teeth, or insufficient bone density, these issues must be addressed first. Patients who smoke heavily or have uncontrolled diabetes may experience higher failure rates and should discuss risk factors with their dentist.

If you have significant bone loss and require implant-supported bridges, you may need bone grafting or sinus lift procedures to ensure adequate support for the implants.

Benefits of Dental Bridges

Choosing dental bridges offers numerous advantages that improve both your oral health and quality of life. Understanding these benefits helps you appreciate the value of this time-tested restoration.

Natural-Looking Aesthetic Results

Modern dental bridges are custom-crafted to match the color, shape, and size of your natural teeth, creating a seamless, beautiful smile. Porcelain and ceramic materials closely mimic the translucency and appearance of tooth enamel, making bridges virtually indistinguishable from your real teeth. 

Improved Chewing and Speaking Ability

Dental bridges restore your ability to bite and chew comfortably, allowing you to enjoy a balanced, healthy diet. Bridges also fill gaps that affect speech, improving pronunciation and clarity so you can communicate without self-consciousness.

More Affordable Than Implants

Dental bridges generally cost significantly less upfront than dental implants, making them a more budget-friendly tooth replacement option. While implants may last longer, bridges offer excellent value for patients seeking durable, functional restorations without the higher initial investment.

Prevents Complications from Missing Teeth

Beyond aesthetics, bridges prevent problems that occur when teeth are missing. They stop adjacent teeth from drifting into empty spaces, maintain proper tooth alignment, avoid bite problems, and reduce the risk of TMJ disorders. You protect your remaining natural teeth and oral health by immediately addressing tooth loss with bridges.

The Dental Bridge Process at Redstone Family Dental

Understanding each step of the dental bridge procedure helps you feel prepared and confident throughout your treatment.

Step 1: Consultation and Examination

Your journey begins with a comprehensive consultation where we assess your oral health, discuss your concerns, and determine if a dental bridge is the right solution. We’ll take digital X-rays or 3D scans to evaluate adjacent teeth, bone structure, and gum health. We’ll explain the types of bridges available, review the timeline, and provide a detailed cost estimate.​

Step 2: Preparing the Abutment Teeth

At your next appointment, we will prepare the abutment teeth that will support your bridge by carefully reshaping them and removing a small amount of enamel to create space for the crowns. The procedure is performed under local anesthesia to ensure your comfort. For Maryland bridges, minimal tooth preparation is needed.​

Step 3: Impressions and Temporary Bridge

After tooth preparation, we take precise impressions of your teeth using traditional molds or digital scanning technology. While your permanent bridge is being fabricated in our dental laboratory (2-3 weeks), we place a temporary bridge to protect your prepared teeth and maintain aesthetics and function.​

Step 4: Final Bridge Placement

Once your permanent bridge is ready, we remove the temporary bridge, clean your prepared teeth thoroughly, and carefully place the permanent bridge to check fit, color, and bite alignment. If any adjustments are needed, we make them on the spot. When everything looks and feels perfect, we permanently cement the bridge in place using dental adhesive.​

Our team at Redstone Family Dental prioritizes your comfort and satisfaction throughout the process.

What to Expect After Getting a Dental Bridge

Knowing what to expect during the adjustment period helps ensure a smooth recovery and long-term success with your new dental bridge.

Initial Adjustment

It’s normal to experience slight tenderness, sensitivity to hot or cold foods, and an unfamiliar feeling in your mouth immediately after bridge placement. These sensations generally diminish within the first two weeks as your mouth adapts. During the first few days, stick to soft foods and gradually reintroduce firmer foods as comfort allows. Avoid extremely hard, sticky, or chewy foods that could dislodge or damage your new bridge.​

Caring for Your Bridge

Proper care is essential for the longevity of your bridge. Brush at least twice daily using a soft-bristled toothbrush and fluoride toothpaste, paying special attention to where the bridge meets your gums.​

Since regular floss cannot pass under a bridge, use specialized tools like floss threaders, interdental brushes, or water flossers to clean beneath the pontic and remove debris. Use an alcohol-free antibacterial or fluoride mouthwash daily to reduce bacteria.​

Schedule regular dental checkups every six months for professional cleanings and examinations. With proper care and maintenance, your dental bridge can provide many years of reliable function.​

Checklist: What to Ask Your Dentist

  • What type of dental bridge do you recommend for my specific case, and why?
  • How many teeth will the bridge replace, and which teeth will serve as abutments?
  • What materials will be used, and how will they match my natural teeth?
  • Do I need any preparatory treatments before bridge placement?
  • What is the complete timeline from consultation to final placement?
  • What is the total cost, and what's included in that price?
  • Does my insurance cover dental bridges, and can you help verify my benefits?
  • How long can I expect my bridge to last, and what factors affect longevity?
  • What care and maintenance will my bridge require?
  • What are the alternatives to dental bridges, and how do they compare?

Frequently Asked Questions

How long do dental bridges last?

Dental bridges last 5-15 years on average, with many lasting 20 years or longer when properly maintained. Longevity depends on bridge type, materials used, oral hygiene practices, and dietary habits. Implant-supported bridges can last 30+ years, while traditional porcelain bridges average 10-15 years with excellent care. Regular dental checkups, thorough cleaning under the bridge with floss threaders, and avoiding hard or sticky foods significantly extend the lifespan of the bridge.​

Is getting a dental bridge painful?

The dental bridge procedure is performed under local anesthesia, so you won’t feel pain during tooth preparation or bridge placement. Afterward, you may experience mild tenderness or sensitivity for a few days, which can be managed with over-the-counter pain relievers. Most patients report that discomfort is minimal and subsides quickly within about two weeks.​

Can I eat normally with a dental bridge?

Yes! A well-fitted dental bridge restores your ability to chew comfortably and eat most foods. Once you’ve adjusted to your bridge (approximately 2 weeks), you can enjoy a regular diet. However, we recommend avoiding extremely sticky foods like caramel and very hard foods like ice or hard candies that could damage the bridge. With proper care, your bridge will function much like your natural teeth.​

How do dental bridges compare to dental implants?

Both bridges and implants effectively replace missing teeth, but differ in several ways. Bridges are generally faster (2-3 weeks vs. 3-6 months), less expensive upfront, and don’t require surgery. However, bridges require altering adjacent healthy teeth and don’t prevent jawbone loss. Implants are independent restorations that preserve bone and last longer but cost more initially. Our Calgary dentists can help you decide which option best fits your needs and budget.​

Will my dental bridge look natural?

Absolutely! Modern dental bridges are custom-crafted using high-quality porcelain or ceramic materials that closely mimic natural tooth enamel. We carefully match your bridge to the shade of your surrounding teeth, ensuring a seamless result. When properly designed and placed, bridges are virtually indistinguishable from your natural teeth.​

How do I clean my dental bridge?

Caring for your bridge requires daily attention. Brush at least twice daily with fluoride toothpaste, paying special attention to where the bridge meets your gums. Use floss threaders, interdental brushes, or a water flosser to clean beneath the pontic. Rinse daily with fluoride or antibacterial mouthwash. Schedule professional cleanings every six months.​

Can a dental bridge be repaired?

Minor damage, like small chips, may be repairable in some cases. However, extensive damage, loosening, or underlying decay requires bridge replacement. Regular dental checkups help identify issues early before they become serious problems. With proper maintenance, you can maximize your bridge’s lifespan and avoid premature replacement.​
